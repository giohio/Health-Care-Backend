## Continuing Education Activity

Iron deficiency imposes a substantial global burden, affecting more than 2 billion people worldwide. It is estimated that iron deficiency affects about 40% of the population in developing countries and 10% in developed countries. It is the most common cause of anemia worldwide. Iron is essential for multiple important biological functions, including the synthesis of hemoglobin and myoglobin, oxygen transport, hormone synthesis, cell regulation/proliferation, DNA synthesis, mitochondrial electron transport, and antioxidation.It also plays a vital role in regulating both innate and adaptive immunity.

The etiologies of chronic iron deficiency vary widely by demographics such as gender, age, geographic region, and diet.The primary cause of iron deficiency worldwide is a lack of enough iron-containing food. Less common causes include blood loss, gastrointestinal malabsorption, and genetic conditions. Quantifiable iron deficiency can result from inadequate intake, impaired absorption, or blood loss. Iron deficiency can be with and without anemia, both of which have clinical implications. Inflammatory states can also cause functional iron deficiency, or anemia of chronic disease, in which iron stores are adequate, but are sequestered by macrophages and unavailable for systemic use. This activity helps delineate the etiologies, clinical presentations, and appropriate treatments of iron-deficiency anemia.

**Objectives:**

*   Identify the etiology of iron deficiency anemia.

*   Evaluate the presentation of a patient with iron deficiency anemia.

*   Implement the treatment and management options available for iron deficiency anemia.

*   Communicate the importance of improving care coordination among interprofessional team members to improve outcomes for patients affected by iron deficiency anemia.

## Introduction

Anemia is defined as hemoglobin below two standard deviations of the mean for the age and gender of the patient. Iron is an essential component of the hemoglobin molecule. The most common cause of anemia worldwide is iron deficiency, which results in microcytic, hypochromic red blood cells on peripheral smear. Causes of iron deficiency vary based on age, gender, and socioeconomic status. The patient often presents with nonspecific complaints, such as fatigue and dyspnea on exertion. Diagnosis relies on laboratory assessment, typically with low hemoglobin and low ferritin, although ferritin is also elevated in the presence of inflammation. Treatment involves reversing the underlying condition and providing iron supplementation. Iron supplementation is most often oral, but certain patients may require intravenous iron. Patients with iron-deficient anemia have been found to have longer hospital stays, along with a higher number of adverse events.[[1]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[2]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[3]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[4]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)

## Etiology

Iron-deficiency anemia may result from insufficient iron intake, impaired iron absorption, or chronic blood loss. These factors may vary by age, gender, and socioeconomic status. In older adults, the most common cause of iron-deficiency anemia is chronic blood loss. Important causes of chronic blood loss include gastrointestinal malignancy, peptic ulcer disease, inflammatory bowel disease, celiac disease, and various lesions within the gastrointestinal tract. Additionally, chronic blood loss may also result from menstrual bleeding or other non-gastrointestinal sources.

Iron-deficiency anemia can also occur during periods of increased iron demand, such as pregnancy, infancy, and adolescence, when the body's need for iron surpasses intake or absorption. Conditions that decrease iron absorption, such as celiac disease, atrophic gastritis, and post-bariatric surgery, can also contribute to this condition.[[5]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)

Chronic inflammatory conditions, including chronic kidney disease, heart failure, and inflammatory bowel disease, can impair iron absorption or utilization, often through mechanisms involving hepcidin, which sequesters iron and reduces its availability for erythropoiesis.[[5]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#) Iron-deficiency anemia is prevalent in neonates and young children who are not breastfed and instead consume cow's milk.

In developing countries, significant causes of iron-deficiency anemia include parasitic infections (eg, hookworm), malaria, and inherited hemoglobinopathies, which often coexist with nutritional deficiencies and chronic inflammation.[[6]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#) Helicobacter pylori infection and autoimmune gastritis are increasingly recognized as causes of refractory or unexplained iron-deficiency anemia, particularly when standard gastrointestinal evaluations fail to reveal the underlying cause. Rarely, hereditary disorders that affect iron metabolism, such as iron-refractory iron-deficiency anemia (IRIDA), should be considered, particularly in cases that do not respond to conventional treatments.[[7]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[8]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[9]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[10]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)

## Epidemiology

According to the Global Burden of Disease Study 2021, the disease-adjusted life years (DALYs) for iron deficiency increased between 1990 and 2021, with the highest burden seen in low socioeconomic areas.[[11]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)An estimated 14% of adults in the United States have iron-deficiency anemia, according to the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES).[[5]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)The prevalence of iron deficiency among nonpregnant US adults varies by the ferritin threshold. It is estimated at 5.9 million using a threshold of 15 ng/ml, but increases to 3.3 million when the threshold is set at 45 ng/ml.[[12]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)

In the US, iron deficiency is seen in 9% of toddlers aged 1 to 2 years. Compared to white toddlers, Hispanic toddlers are twice as likely to have an iron deficiency.The prevalence in adolescent girls and women in the reproductive age group is between 9% to 11%. Iron deficiency is most common in multiparous women from low-income minority populations. In males, it is observed in approximately 1% of the population, with a slightly higher prevalence of 2% to 4% in middle-aged and older men.[[13]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[14]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)There is a positive correlation between obesity and the risk of developing iron deficiency, as adipocytes produce hepcidin.[[5]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[15]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)

The American Gastroenterological Association emphasizes that iron deficiency is the most common nutritional deficiency and recommends evaluation for underlying causes, especially gastrointestinal blood loss or malabsorption, in adults presenting with iron-deficiency anemia. The global burden remains substantial, with a higher prevalence in low- and middle-income countries and among women and children.[[4]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[16]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)

## Pathophysiology

Iron-deficiency anemia is characterized by diminished hemoglobin production resulting from inadequate iron supply for erythropoiesis. It begins with the depletion of iron storage (ferritin), which is primarily found in the liver and the reticuloendothelial system. As iron reserves diminish, there is a subsequent decline in circulating iron (transferrin) levels, resulting in decreased hemoglobin synthesis within the bone marrow.[[17]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[18]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)

Iron is crucial for heme synthesis, the oxygen-carrying component of hemoglobin.When iron intake is inadequate, absorption is impaired (eg, celiac disease, post-bariatric surgery), or losses are excessive (eg, gastrointestinal bleeding, heavy menstrual bleeding), iron stores become depleted.As stores are exhausted, serum ferritin decreases, followed by a drop in serum iron and transferrin saturation, while total iron-binding capacity (TIBC) increases as a compensatory response.[[5]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)

Once iron supply is insufficient to meet erythropoietic demand, erythroblasts in the bone marrow cannot synthesize adequate hemoglobin, resulting in the production of smaller (microcytic), hypochromic red blood cells.This leads to the clinical manifestation of anemia.Hormone hepcidin, which regulates iron absorption and release from macrophages, is suppressed in absolute iron deficiency, promoting increased intestinal iron absorption and mobilization from stores. The American Gastroenterological Association emphasizes that iron-deficiency anemia is most commonly caused by chronic blood loss, impaired absorption, or increased requirements, and recommends evaluation for underlying causes, particularly gastrointestinal sources, in adults without apparent blood loss.[[17]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[19]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[20]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)

## Histopathology

Several distinct features on peripheral blood smear and bone marrow examination characterize the histopathology of iron-deficiency anemia.On peripheral smear, the hallmark findings are microcytosis(red blood cells smaller than usual) and hypochromia(increased central pallor due to reduced hemoglobin content).Additional morphologic features include anisocytosis(variation in red cell size),poikilocytosis(variation in red cell shape), and the presence of pencil cells(elongated, cigar-shaped erythrocytes).Prekeratocytes (cells with a single, small cytoplasmic projection) are also more frequently observed in iron-deficiency anemia compared to other microcytic anemias such as β-thalassemia minor or anemia of chronic disease. Bone marrow examination, though rarely required for diagnosis, typically reveals erythroid hyperplasia with a predominance of early erythroid precursors and a marked reduction or absence of stainable iron in macrophages.[[21]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[22]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)

## History and Physical

The symptoms and signs of iron-deficiency anemia include both general features of anemia and findings more specific to iron deficiency. According to the American Society of Hematology, common symptoms are fatigue, dyspnea, and syncope, which are typical of all anemias. Additional features more specific to iron deficiency include alopecia, glossitis, pica, and decreased cognitive abilities, attention, and concentration. Physical findings, while often subtle, can include pallor, dry mouth, atrophic glossitis, angular cheilitis, and hair loss.Chronic or severe cases may lead to koilonychia(spoon-shaped nails) and, rarely, Plummer–Vinson syndrome. Cardiovascular signs such as tachycardia, functional systolic murmur, and, in severe cases, syncope or heart failure exacerbation may be observed, particularly when anemia is profound or develops rapidly.

Symptoms may be absent or minimal in early or mild disease, and the rate of hemoglobin decline, age, and comorbidities (eg, chronic kidney disease, heart failure, and inflammatory bowel disease) influence the clinical picture. Notably, iron deficiency without anemia can still cause symptoms such as fatigue, cognitive impairment, and restless leg syndrome.[[5]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[23]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[24]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)

## Evaluation

The initial evaluation of anemia starts with confirming the condition, defined as having a hemoglobin level of less than 13 g/dL in men and less than 12 g/dL in nonpregnant women. The hemoglobin indices in iron deficiency will demonstrate a low mean corpuscular hemoglobin and mean corpuscular hemoglobin volume. Hematoscopy shows microcytosis, hypochromia, and anisocytosis, as reflected by a red cell distribution width higher than the reference range. Serum levels of ferritin, iron, and transferrin saturation will be decreased. The American Gastroenterological Association (AGA) and the American Society of Hematology recommend that a serum ferritin level of less than 45 ng/mL be used to diagnose iron-deficiency anemia. This threshold provides the optimal sensitivity and specificity for diagnosing iron-deficiency anemia.[[4]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[20]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)

In cases of inflammation or chronic illness, including malignancies, infections, autoimmune diseases, and collagen diseases, ferritin levels may be inappropriately elevated. The standard for establishing iron deficiency is a bone marrow aspiration or biopsy followed by iron staining, since it is unaffected by inflammation. Due to the high cost and invasiveness of this test, it is rarely performed.[[8]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/)

Measurement of other biomarkers, such as soluble transferrin receptor (sTfR), hepcidin, and reticulocyte hemoglobin content (CHr/Ret-He), helps distinguish iron-deficiency anemia from anemia of inflammation and facilitates early detection and more accurate monitoring of iron status.

Once iron deficiency is confirmed, the next step is to identify the underlying cause.In premenopausal women, a common cause of anemia is menstrual blood loss. In contrast, gastrointestinal blood loss and malabsorption are more common in men and postmenopausal women. The AGA recommends bidirectional endoscopy (esophagogastroduodenoscopy and colonoscopy) for men and postmenopausal women with IDA when no other clear source is identified. Additional evaluation for celiac disease or _Helicobacter pylori_ infection may be warranted in select cases.[[23]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)

## Treatment / Management

Iron-deficiency anemia is managed with a focus on both iron repletion and the correction of the underlying cause of deficiency.The American Gastroenterological Association recommends oral iron supplementation as first-line therapy, typically with ferrous sulfate, due to its efficacy, availability, and low cost.Standard dosing is 100-200 mg elemental iron daily; lower or alternate-day dosing may improve tolerability and absorption.Oral iron should be taken on an empty stomach to maximize absorption, but gastrointestinal side effects (nausea, constipation) are common and may limit adherence.Co-administration with vitamin C may enhance absorption, though evidence is limited.A hematologic response is typically observed within 1 month; if not, assessment for nonadherence, malabsorption, or ongoing blood loss should be performed.

Intravenous iron is indicated for patients who cannot tolerate oral iron, have malabsorption syndromes (eg, celiac disease, post-bariatric surgery), chronic inflammatory conditions (eg, chronic kidney disease, heart failure, inflammatory bowel disease, cancer), ongoing blood loss, or require rapid repletion (eg, preoperative patients, pregnancy in the second or third trimester).Modern IV formulations (eg, ferric derisomaltose, ferric carboxymaltose, iron isomaltoside, and ferumoxytol) permit high-dose, single-infusion administration with good safety profiles but should be administered in settings equipped to manage rare hypersensitivity reactions and unique adverse effects, such as hypophosphatemia with ferric carboxymaltose.

Emerging oral formulations, including nano-iron supplements and metal-polyphenol network-based iron supplements, may improve absorption and reduce gastrointestinal toxicity, but further clinical validation is ongoing.[[25]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)

Red blood cell transfusion is reserved for severe, symptomatic anemia with hemodynamic instability and is not a routine treatment for iron-deficiency anemia.Management also requires identification and treatment of the underlying cause(eg, gastrointestinal bleeding, menstrual losses, dietary insufficiency), as iron repletion alone is insufficient if the source of iron loss persists.[[4]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[5]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[20]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[26]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[23]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)

## Differential Diagnosis

The differential diagnosis of iron-deficiency anemia includes the following:

*   Lead poisoning

*   Microcytic anemia

*   Anemia of chronic disease

*   Hemoglobin CC disease

*   Hemoglobin DD disease

*   Autoimmune hemolytic anemia

*   Hemoglobin S-beta thalassemia

## Pertinent Studies and Ongoing Trials

**Guidelines**

The United States Preventive Services Task Force has concluded that the evidence is insufficient to assess the balance of benefits and harms of screening for iron-deficiency anemia in pregnant women and children aged 6 to 24 months.[[27]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)

**Outcomes**

Most studies on iron replacement therapies were done several decades ago. Many of these studies were not randomized, and the follow-up appointments were short. Reports on the treatment of iron-deficiency anemia are based on expert opinion, but there is a clear short-term benefit.[[28]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)There remain significant gaps in the duration of treatment, as well as in ethnic differences in responses to iron and in risk factors for adverse reactions.

## Prognosis

The prognosis of iron-deficiency anemia is generally favorable if the underlying cause is identified and treated appropriately and if iron stores are sufficiently replenished. Most patients experience symptom improvement and anemia resolution with treatment. However, the prognosis can vary based on several factors. Even with thorough investigation and management, a significant number of patients may experience persistent or recurrent anemia, especially if the underlying cause is not completely addressed or if there are additional health issues, such as chronic kidney disease, heart failure, or cancer.Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcomes (KDIGO) and other organizations have issued updated guidelines for anemia of chronic kidney disease, recommending early iron supplementation and consideration of new agents, such as hypoxia-inducible factor–prolyl hydroxylase inhibitors (HIF-PHI), for select groups. In high-risk or frail populations, untreated or recurrent iron-deficiency anemia can lead to increased morbidity, a decreased quality of life, and, in some cases, a higher risk of mortality.[[4]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[5]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[20]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)

## Complications

Untreated iron-deficiency anemia can lead to organ dysfunction, affecting the hematologic, neurologic, cardiovascular, epithelial, reproductive, and immunologic systems. Neurologic complications may include restless legs syndrome, depression, and headaches. In rare cases, chronic iron-deficiency anemia may lead to Plummer-Vinson syndrome. Cardiovascular complications can manifest as tachycardia, functional systolic murmurs, worsening heart failure, and, in severe instances, cardiovascular collapse. Immune dysfunction resulting from iron deficiency can increase susceptibility to infections. In women, it can contribute to infertility and adverse pregnancy outcomes. In children, iron-deficiency anemia can affect neurodevelopment, leading to attention-deficit disorder, poor academic performance, and growth impairment.[[5]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[29]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)[[30]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)

## Postoperative and Rehabilitation Care

Individuals with severe iron-deficiency anemia should limit highly strenuous physical activity until the anemia has been corrected. March hemoglobinuria can also lead to iron-deficiency anemia and may require modifications to footwear or physical activity.

## Consultations

The following should be consulted in iron-deficiency anemia:

*   Surgeon, if there is a surgical gastrointestinal cause (rare)

*   Gastroenterologist for endoscopy and localization of gastrointestinal tract bleeding, if needed

*   Radiologist, if the bleeding is brisk and can be managed by embolization

*   Hematologist, if anemia is not improving with simple iron supplementation

## Deterrence and Patient Education

Populations at high risk should be considered for prophylaxis with iron therapy. These groups include women with heavy menstrual cycles, frequent blood donors, adolescent girls, and people who eat strict vegetarian diets. Empirical iron supplements for everyone are not recommended, as there is no evidence that this is beneficial and may be harmful.

## Pearls and Other Issues

Key points to consider regarding iron-deficiency anemia are as follows:

*   Patients often present with nonspecific symptoms and anemia; a careful history and physical examination are required.

*   In the pediatric population, routine screening beginning at 9 to 12 months and repeated annually thereafter has helped prevent the development of severe anemia.

*   In the adult population, evaluating the gastrointestinal system as a potential cause of iron-deficient anemia can be a diagnostic challenge. The emerging role of less invasive testing for celiac disease, autoimmune atrophic gastritis, and _Helicobacter pylori_ infections has improved disease recognition and diagnosis. While there is always the potential for relapse after iron supplementation, there is a lack of guidance regarding when to stop iron supplementation. An additional pitfall of iron-deficiency anemia is poorer outcomes across many medical conditions.

*   Some of the adverse effects of patients with iron-deficient anemia are higher mortality, longer hospital stays, and more cardiovascular events.

## Enhancing Healthcare Team Outcomes

Although iron deficiency is among the oldest and most common medical disorders, it can benefit from further clinical attention and evaluation. Its nonspecific symptoms make diagnosis especially challenging. Many children, elderly patients, and pregnant women continue to have undiagnosed iron-deficiency anemia or remain under-treated. Evidence from an interprofessional panel of clinicians indicates that iron-deficiency anemia is prevalent among hospitalized patients. It is associated with poorer outcomes, including longer hospital stays and reduced quality of life. There are also risks for those who receive blood transfusions. The panel has recommended several strategies for the early diagnosis, treatment, and follow-up of these patients.[[31]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448065/#)The most critical recommendation is a prompt referral to a specialist; not all cases of iron-deficiency anemia are attributable solely to gastrointestinal bleeding or heavy menstrual bleeding. The primary care healthcare provider plays a vital role, as they are often the first to recognize iron-deficiency anemia. Others who are essential in detecting iron-deficiency anemia include the following:

*   Laboratory technologists determine serum ferritin, transferrin, vitamin levels, and renal function.

*   Hematologists determine the cause.

*   Pharmacists determine the best formula for iron and the presence of adverse effects. Replacement therapy is either intravenous or oral iron, with red blood cell transfusions reserved for emergencies. Each has its benefits and limitations.

*   Nurses ensure treatment adherence and educate patients about the symptoms and signs of anemia.

*   Internists follow and monitor patients.
