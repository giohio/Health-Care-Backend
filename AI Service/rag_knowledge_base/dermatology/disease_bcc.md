## What is basal cell carcinoma?

Basal cell carcinoma (BCC) is a common, locally invasive, [keratinocyte cancer](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/keratinocyte-cancer) (also known as nonmelanoma cancer). It is the most common form of [skin cancer](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/skin-cancer). BCC is also known as rodent ulcer and basalioma. Patients with BCC often develop multiple primary tumours over time.

BCC is also known as rodent ulcer and basalioma.

## Who gets basal cell carcinoma?

Risk factors for BCC include:

*   Age and sex: BCCs are particularly prevalent in elderly males. However, they also affect females and younger adults
*   Previous BCC or other form of skin cancer ([squamous cell carcinoma](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/cutaneous-squamous-cell-carcinoma), [melanoma](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/melanoma))
*   Sun damage ([photoageing](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/ageing-skin), [actinic keratoses](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/actinic-keratosis))
*   Repeated prior episodes of [sunburn](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/sunburn)
*   Fair skin, blue eyes and blond or red hair — note; BCC can also affect darker [skin types](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/skin-phototype)
*   Previous cutaneous injury, [thermal burn](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/thermal-burn), disease (eg [cutaneous lupus](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/cutaneous-lupus-erythematosus), [sebaceous naevus](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/sebaceous-naevus))
*   Inherited syndromes: BCC is a particular problem for families with basal cell naevus syndrome ([Gorlin syndrome](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/basal-cell-naevus-syndrome)), [Bazex-Dupré-Christol syndrome](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/bazex-dupre-christol-syndrome), Rombo syndrome, Oley syndrome and [xeroderma pigmentosum](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/xeroderma-pigmentosum)
*   Other risk factors include ionising radiation, exposure to [arsenic](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/chronic-arsenic-poisoning), immune suppression due to [disease](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/immunodeficiency) or [medicines](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/drug-induced-immunosuppression), and use of some other medicines such as hydrochlorothiazide.

## What causes basal cell carcinoma?

The cause of BCC is multifactorial.

*   Most often, there are DNA mutations in the patched (PTCH) tumour suppressor gene, part of the hedgehog signalling pathway.
*   These may be triggered by exposure to [ultraviolet radiation](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/ultraviolet-radiation-and-human-health).
*   Various spontaneous and inherited [gene defects](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/genetics-of-basal-cell-carcinoma)predispose to BCC.

## What are the clinical features of basal cell carcinoma?

BCC is a locally invasive skin tumour. The main characteristics are:

*   Slowly growing plaque or nodule
*   Skin coloured, pink or pigmented
*   Varies in size from a few millimetres to several centimetres in diameter
*   Spontaneous bleeding or ulceration.

BCC is very rarely a threat to life. A tiny proportion of BCCs grow rapidly, invade deeply, and/or metastasise to local lymph nodes.

See also: [Basal cell carcinoma in skin of colour](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/basal-cell-carcinoma-in-skin-of-colour)

## Types of basal cell carcinoma

There are several distinct clinical types of BCC, and over 20 histological growth patterns of BCC.

### Nodular BCC

*   Most common type of facial BCC
*   Shiny or pearly nodule with a smooth surface
*   May have central depression or ulceration, so its edges appear rolled
*   Blood vessels cross its surface
*   Cystic variant is soft, with jelly-like contents
*   Micronodular, microcystic and infiltrative types are potentially aggressive subtypes
*   Also known as nodulocystic carcinoma

## What are the complications of basal cell carcinoma?

### Recurrent BCC

Recurrence of BCC after initial treatment is not uncommon. Characteristics of recurrent BCC often include:

*   Incomplete excision or narrow margins at primary excision
*   Morphoeic, micronodular, and infiltrative subtypes
*   Location on head and neck.

### Advanced BCC

Advanced BCCs are large, often neglected tumours.

*   They may be several centimetres in diameter
*   They may be deeply infiltrating into tissues below the skin
*   They are difficult or impossible to treat surgically

### Metastatic BCC

*   Very rare
*   Primary tumour is often large, neglected or recurrent, located on head and neck, with aggressive subtype
*   May have had multiple prior treatments
*   May arise in-site exposed to ionising radiation
*   Can be fatal

## How is basal cell carcinoma diagnosed?

BCC is diagnosed clinically by the presence of a slowly enlarging skin lesion with typical appearance.

### BCC dermoscopy criteria

Menzies’ algorithm (Menzies, 2000)

1.   Absence of pigment network
2.   Presence of ≥1 among:

*   Arborizing vessels (telangiectasia with tree-like branching)
*   Ulceration
*   Blue-gray ovoid nests
*   Blue-gray globules
*   Leaf-like areas
*   Spoke-wheel areas.

Additional criteria for non-pigmented, superficial BCC:

*   Shiny white to red structure-less areas
*   Short fine telangiectasia
*   Multiple small ulcerations (erosions).

Additional non-classical criteria:

*   Concentric structures
*   Blue-gray dots, in focus
*   Melanocytic-like criteria
*   Brown-black dots and globules
*   Blue-white veil-like structures
*   Non-classical vascular patterns (hairpin, glomerular, dotted, comma).

Structures under polarised light:

*   [Shiny white lines](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/perpendicular-white-lines-dermoscopy) — tend to be disorganised or arranged in parallel
*   Shiny white areas (blotches)
*   [Rosettes](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/rosettes)
*   Rainbow pattern.

Additional recent criteria:

*   Stellate pattern — from the visible tumour edge; may be formed by white lines, vessels, or uneven skin surfaces
*   Negative maple leaf-like areas
*   Multiple aggregated yellow-white (MAY) globules.

The diagnosis and histological subtype is usually confirmed pathologically by a diagnostic biopsy or following [excision](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/excision-of-skin-lesions). See: [basal cell carcinoma pathology](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/basal-cell-carcinoma-pathology).

While histology remains the gold standard for BCC subtyping, non-invasive optical techniques like line-field confocal [optical coherence tomography (LC-OCT)](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/optical-coherence-tomography) are increasingly utilized for BCC diagnosis and monitoring. LC-OCT has proven its effectiveness in real-world settings, enabling early detection, minimizing unnecessary biopsies, and providing valuable insights into disease progression, especially in cases where surgery is not indicated.

## What is the treatment for primary basal cell carcinoma?

The treatment for a BCC depends on its type, size and location, the number to be treated, patient factors, and the preference or expertise of the doctor. Most BCCs are treated surgically. Long-term follow-up is recommended to check for new lesions and recurrence; the latter may be unnecessary if histology has reported wide clear margins.

### Excision biopsy

[Excision](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/excision-of-skin-lesions) means the lesion is cut out and the skin stitched up.

*   Most appropriate treatment for nodular, infiltrative and morphoeic BCCs.
*   Should include 3 to 5 mm margin of normal skin around the tumour.
*   Very large lesions may require [flap](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/flaps) or [skin graft](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/skin-grafting) to repair the defect.
*   A pathologist will report deep and lateral margins.
*   Further surgery is recommended for lesions that are incompletely excised.

### Mohs micrographically controlled excision

[Mohs micrographically controlled surgery](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/mohs-micrographic-surgery) involves examining carefully marked excised tissue under the microscope, layer by layer, to ensure complete excision.

*   Very high cure rates achieved by trained Mohs surgeons.
*   Used in high-risk areas of the face around eyes, lips, and nose.
*   Suitable for ill-defined, morphoeic, infiltrative and recurrent subtypes.
*   Large defects are repaired by [flap](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/flaps) or [skin graft](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/skin-grafting).

### Superficial skin surgery

Superficial skin surgery comprises shave, [curettage](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/curettage-and-cautery), and [electrocautery](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/electrosurgery). It is a rapid technique using [local anaesthesia](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/local-anaesthesia) and does not require [sutures](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/suture-materials).

*   Suitable for small, well-defined nodular or superficial BCCs.
*   Lesions are usually located on the trunk or limbs.
*   Wound is left open to heal by [secondary intention](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/wound-closure).
*   Moist [wound dressings](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/synthetic-wound-dressings) lead to healing within a few weeks.
*   Eventual scar quality variable.

### Cryotherapy

[Cryotherapy](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/cryotherapy) is the treatment of a superficial skin lesion by freezing it, usually with liquid nitrogen.

*   Suitable for small superficial BCCs on covered areas of the trunk and limbs.
*   Best avoided for BCCs on head and neck, and distal to knees.
*   Double freeze-thaw technique.
*   Results in a blister that crusts over and heals within several weeks.
*   Leaves permanent white mark.

### Photodynamic therapy

[Photodynamic therapy](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/photodynamic-therapy) (PDT) refers to a technique in which BCC is treated with a photosensitising chemical, and exposed to light several hours later.

*   Topical photosensitisers include aminolevulinic acid lotion and [methyl aminolevulinate cream](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/methyl-aminolevulinate-photodynamic-therapy).
*   Suitable for low-risk small, superficial BCCs.
*   Best avoided if tumour in-site at high risk of recurrence.
*   Results in inflammatory reaction, maximal 3–4 days after procedure.
*   Treatment repeated 7 days after initial treatment.
*   Excellent cosmetic results.

### Imiquimod cream

[Imiquimod](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/imiquimod) is an immune response modifier.

*   Best used for superficial BCCs less than 2 cm diameter.
*   Applied three to five times each week, for 6–16 weeks.
*   Results in a variable inflammatory reaction, maximal at three weeks.
*   Minimal scarring is usual.

### Fluorouracil cream

[5-Fluorouracil cream](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/5-fluorouracil-cream) is a topical cytotoxic agent.

*   Used to treat small superficial basal cell carcinomas.
*   Requires prolonged course, eg twice daily for 6–12 weeks.
*   Causes inflammatory reaction.
*   Has high recurrence rates.

### Radiotherapy

[Radiotherapy](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/radiotherapy-for-malignant-skin-diseases) or X-ray treatment can be used to treat primary BCCs or as adjunctive treatment if margins are incomplete.

*   Mainly used if surgery is not suitable.
*   Best avoided in young patients and in genetic conditions predisposing to skin cancer.
*   Best cosmetic results achieved using multiple fractions.
*   Typically, patient attends once-weekly for several weeks.
*   Causes inflammatory reaction followed by scar.
*   Risk of [radiodermatitis](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/radiation-dermatitis), late recurrence, and new tumours.

## What is the treatment for advanced or metastatic basal cell carcinoma?

Locally advanced primary, recurrent or metastatic BCC requires multidisciplinary consultation. Often a combination of treatments is used.

*   [Surgery](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/the-role-of-surgery-in-dermatology)
*   [Radiotherapy](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/radiotherapy-for-malignant-skin-diseases)
*   [Targeted therapy](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/targeted-cancer-therapies).

Targeted therapy refers to the hedgehog signalling pathway inhibitors, [vismodegib](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/vismodegib) and [sonidegib](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/sonidegib). These drugs have some important risks and side effects.

## How can basal cell carcinoma be prevented?

The most important way to prevent BCC is to avoid [sunburn](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/sunburn). This is especially important in childhood and early life. Fair-skinned individuals and those with a personal or family history of BCC should [protect their skin from sun exposure](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/sun-protection) daily, year-round and lifelong.

*   Stay indoors or under the shade in the middle of the day.
*   Wear [covering clothing](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/sun-protective-clothing).
*   Apply high protection factor SPF50+ broad-spectrum [sunscreens](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/topical-sunscreen-agents) generously to exposed skin if outdoors.
*   Avoid [indoor tanning](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/sunbeds-and-solaria).

Oral [nicotinamide](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/nicotinamide) (vitamin B3) in a dose of 500 mg twice daily may reduce the number and severity of BCCs.

## What is the outlook for basal cell carcinoma?

Most BCCs are cured by treatment. Cure is most likely if treatment is undertaken when the lesion is small.

About 50% of people with BCC develop a second one within 3 years of the first. They are also at increased risk of other [skin cancers](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/skin-cancer), especially [melanoma](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/melanoma). Regular [self-skin examinations](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/self-skin-examination) and long-term annual [skin checks](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/see-your-doctor-for-a-skin-check) by an experienced health professional are recommended.
